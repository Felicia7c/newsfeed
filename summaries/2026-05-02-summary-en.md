**Background**: Many USB-C cables include an embedded eMarker chip that stores and reports properties such as supported data rates and maximum current, and it communicates over USB-C mechanisms (e.g., via VCONN/USB-PD related signaling). USB Power Delivery (USB PD) enables a charger/device to negotiate voltage and current, so the cable’s rated current and features can constrain achievable wattage. Higher-speed USB and Thunderbolt/USB4 modes have stricter cable requirements, which is why two USB-C cables can behave very differently even if they look the same.
