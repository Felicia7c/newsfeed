A GrapheneOS-linked discussion argues that hardware attestation (e.g., TPM/phone attestation) is increasingly used to gate services behind “approved” devices and can enable persistent user tracking. Commenters highlight that many real-world attestation deployments lack privacy-preserving designs such as zero-knowledge proofs or blind signatures. If attestation becomes a default requirement for access, it can shift control from users to platform operators and silicon vendors, undermining open ecosystems and alternative OS/device choices. Privacy-wise, linkable attestation artifacts can turn routine logins and transactions into cross-service identifiers. Typical remote attestation relies on hardware-rooted keys and measured-boot evidence (e.g., PCR “quotes”) to convince a verifier about device/software state, which can also be repurposed for gatekeeping. Privacy-preserving approaches exist in the literature, such as Direct Anonymous Attestation (DAA), but commenters argue mainstream deployments often remain linkable in practice.

hackernews · ChuckMcM · May 10, 17:54

**Background**: Hardware attestation is a mechanism where a device proves to a remote party that it is genuine and/or running expected software, often using a TPM and measurements collected during boot. The verifier checks signed measurements (a “quote”) against expected values to decide whether to grant access. A privacy concern is that if the proof is tied to a unique device key or produces linkable transcripts, it can enable tracking across sessions and services. DAA was proposed as a way for TPM-like hardware to produce attestations without revealing a stable device identity to the verifier.
